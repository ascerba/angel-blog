Antonio's Ballad

Happy Valentine's day everyone. I wanted to use the special occasion to share my favorite poem. This is a ballad written by Miguel de Cervantes in his magnum opus Don Quixote, specifically Book 1, Part 2, Chapter XI. Written in the style of a traditional Spanish romance with 8 syllable lines and rhyming every second line. This is Ormsby's English translation of Cervantes's original Spanish ballad. I particularly enjoy stanzas 6-9 (no joke) since those were the ones that make me most nostalgic as they were the only parts I could understand when I first read it at the age of sixteen. I hope you enjoy it as much as I have over the years. Hopefully you all find your own valiente Don Quijote or hermosa Dulcinea del Toboso.

Thou dost love me well, Olalla;
Well I know it, even though
Love's mute tongues, thine eyes, have never
By their glances told me so.

For I know my love thou knowest;
Therefore thine to claim I dare:
Once it ceases to be secret,
Love need never feel despair.

True it is Olalla, sometime
Thou hast all too plainly shown
That thy heart is brass in hardness,
And thy snowy bosom stone.

Yet for all that, in thy coyness,
And thy fickle fits between,
Hope is there—at least the border
of her garment may be seen.

Lures to faith are they, those glimpses,
And to faith in thee I hold;
Kindness cannot make it stronger,
Coldness cannot make it cold.

If it be that love is gentle,
In thy gentleness I see
Something holding out assurance
To the hope of winning thee.

If it be that in devotion
Lies a power hearts to move,
That which every day I show thee,
Helpful to my suit should prove.

Many a time thou must have noticed—
If to notice thou dost care—
How I go about on Monday
Dressed in all my Sunday wear.

Love's eyes love to look on brightness;
Love loves what is gaily dressed;
Sunday, Monday, all I care is
Thou shouldst see me in my best.

No account I make of dances,
Or of strains that pleased thee so,
Keeping thee awake from midnight
Till the cocks began to crow;

Or of how I roundly swore it
That there's none so fair as thou;
True it is, but as I said it,
By the girls I'm hated now.

For Teresa of the hillside
At my praise of thee was sore;
Said, "You think you love an angel;
It's a monkey you adore;

"Caught by all her glittering trinkets,
And her borrowed braids of hair,
And a host of made-up beauties
That would Love himself ensnare."

'Twas a lie, and so I told her,
And her cousin at the word
Gave me his defiance for it;
And what followed thou hast heard.

Mine is no high-flown affection,
Mine no passion par amours—
As they call it—what I offer
Is an honest love, and pure.

Binding cords the holy Church has,
Cords of softest silk they be;
Put thy neck beneath the yolk, dear;
Mine will follow, thou wilt see.

Else—and once for all I swear it
By the saint of most renown—
If I ever quit the mountains,
'Twill be in a friar's gown...

The Trial

Hello, everyone! I had the pleasure of reading The Trial (Der Prozess in Deutsch) by Franz Kafka last week, and it lives up entirely to its reputation of a dreary, esoteric tone. Something that did surprize me about the book was its readability. I had a rough experience with readability with my translation The Metamorphosis, Kafka's other most famous work, in high school, so I expected this book to be just as archaic and needlessly wordy, but no! Everything was simple to understand. The novel was never actually finished, so there are a few noticeable gaps in the narrative. I sincerely recommend this book to anyone that has the stomach to read gloomy books, especially now of all times.

Warning! Spoilers ahead:
One of the first things I picked up on was how much I hated Josef K.. He was an arrogant chauvinist that justified his disgusting behavior with his own inflated ego. K. tried to act like a schemer vying to get his way, and I think that is what added to his defeat and the end of the novel. He kept thinking that he could overcome the court the same way he had risen in business, so he would keep making the wrong decisions in trying to deal with the court. He imagines the court to be a humanly thing that can be overcome with debate and persuassion, but this is crazy Kafka land where the court is this mystical entity that occupies every weird attic in the city and has some kind of tangential connection to every character near K. in the novel. What I think K. is going through in the novel is his acknowledgement of his guilt as a human being. The court sees him and all crimes across his life and cannot be reasoned with. He tried to get out of his dilemma as much as possible meeting three different lady friends on the way (I am not clever enough to figure out the meaning of those three women yet). K. is slowly becoming to the court as Block was to his lawyer. By the end of the novel, he is tired of fighting after a year of struggle: "There would be nothing heroic in resistance, in making trouble for these men, in trying to enjoy a final vestige of life by fighting back." The K. at the beginning of the novel would never have been so resigned to his fate The court had finally broken his spirits, and he had become a dog just like Block. He gets slaughtered with the knife (1914 Prague had no 8th amendment, I guess) and tried to find some kind of meaning or justification for his demise to comfort him but cannot. K. has his emotional climax (or abyss in this situation) when he sees that his failure is being watched by the figure in the window as he is being slaughtered. A truly disheartening book :-(

I know my analysis is probably very surface level, but this is what I have been able to take from the book on my own. This is my blog, not yours, lol. I will likely write about the parable and the ladies in some coming weeks. If you agree or disagree with my take or have some wild card third thing to say, let me know! Email me whenever.
